AP Calculus BC: Unit 6 - Integration and Accumulation of Change

Introduction

Unit 6 focuses on understanding integration and its applications in accumulating change. We will explore definite and indefinite integrals, techniques of integration, and how they are used to calculate areas, volumes, and solve differential equations.

Topics Covered

  • Antiderivatives and Indefinite Integrals
  • Techniques of Integration
  • Definite Integrals and the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus
  • Applications of Integrals

1. Antiderivatives and Indefinite Integrals

The antiderivative of a function \( f(x) \) is a function \( F(x) \) such that \( F'(x) = f(x) \). The indefinite integral represents the family of all antiderivatives of \( f(x) \).

Example: Find the indefinite integral of \( f(x) = 3x^2 \).

                    ∫ 3x² dx = x³ + C

where \( C \) represents the constant of integration.

2. Techniques of Integration

  • Substitution
  • Integration by Parts
  • Partial Fraction Decomposition

Substitution

Used when an integral contains a function and its derivative.

Example: \(\int 2x \sqrt{x^2+1} \, dx \) 

                    Let u = x² + 1, then du = 2x dx.
                    ∫ 2x sqrt(x²+1) dx = ∫ sqrt(u) du = (2/3) u^(3/2) + C = (2/3) (x² + 1)^(3/2) + C

Integration by Parts

Based on the product rule for differentiation: \(\int u \, dv = uv - \int v \, du \).

Example: \(\int x e^x \, dx \)

                    Let u = x, dv = e^x dx,
                    then du = dx, v = e^x.
                    ∫ x e^x dx = x e^x - ∫ e^x dx = x e^x - e^x + C

Partial Fraction Decomposition

Used for rational functions where the degree of the numerator is less than the degree of the denominator.

Example: \(\int \frac{2x}{x^2-1} \, dx \)

                    \(\frac{2x}{x²-1} = \frac{2x}{(x-1)(x+1)} = \frac{A}{x-1} + \frac{B}{x+1}\)
                    Solving, A = 1, B = 1,
                    ∫ (1/(x-1) + 1/(x+1)) dx = ln|x-1| + ln|x+1| + C

3. Definite Integrals and the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

The definite integral represents the accumulation of a quantity, often area under a curve.

Formula: \(\int_{a}^{b} f(x) \, dx = F(b) - F(a)\), where \(F(x)\) is an antiderivative of \(f(x)\).

Example: \(\int_{0}^{2} 3x^2 \, dx \)

                    F(x) = x^3
                    F(2) - F(0) = 2^3 - 0^3 = 8

4. Applications of Integrals

  • Area Between Curves
  • Volume of Solids of Revolution
  • Solving Differential Equations

Area Between Curves

The area between \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) from \(a\) to \(b\) is given by \(\int_{a}^{b} [f(x) - g(x)] \, dx\)

Example: Find the area between \(y = x^2\) and \(y = x + 2\) from \(x = -1\) to \(x = 2\).

                    A = ∫_{-1}^{2} [(x + 2) - (x²)] dx
                    = ∫_{-1}^{2} (x + 2 - x²) dx
                    = [0.5x² + 2x - (1/3)x³]_{-1}^{2} 
                    = [2 - 4/3 - (-0.5 + 2 + 1/3)] 
                    = 9/2

Volume of Solids of Revolution

Volume by the disk method: \(V = \pi \int_{a}^{b} [R(x)]^2 \, dx\)

Example: Find the volume when \(y = \sqrt{x}\) is revolved around the x-axis from \(x = 0\) to \(x = 4\).

                    V = \pi ∫_{0}^{4} [sqrt(x)]² dx
                    = \pi ∫_{0}^{4} x dx
                    = \pi [0.5x²]_{0}^{4}
                    = \pi * 8
                    = 8π

Solving Differential Equations

Solve separable differential equations by separating and integrating both sides.

Example: Solve \(\frac{dy}{dx} = y \sin(x)\)

                    Separate: \(\frac{dy}{y} = \sin(x) dx\)
                    Integrate: \(\ln|y| = -\cos(x) + C\)
                    Solve for y: \(y = e^{-\cos(x) + C} = Ce^{-\cos(x)}\)
