AP Calculus BC: Unit 10 - Infinite Sequences and Series

1. Sequences

A sequence is an ordered list of numbers that often follow a specific pattern. Formally, a sequence is a function whose domain is the set of natural numbers.

Example:

Given the sequence \(a_n = \frac{1}{n}\), find the first 3 terms.

Solution:

a_1 = \frac{1}{1} = 1
a_2 = \frac{1}{2} = 0.5
a_3 = \frac{1}{3} \approx 0.333

2. Series and Partial Sums

A series is the sum of the terms of a sequence. The partial sum is the sum of the first \(n\) terms of the series.

Example:

Given the series \( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2} \), find the partial sum \(S_3\).

Solution:

S_3 = \frac{1}{1^2} + \frac{1}{2^2} + \frac{1}{3^2} = 1 + \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{9} \approx 1 + 0.25 + 0.111 = 1.361

3. Convergence and Divergence of Series

A series converges if the sequence of its partial sums approaches a finite limit. Otherwise, it diverges.

Example:

Does the series \( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n} \) converge or diverge?

Solution:

This is the harmonic series, which is known to diverge.

4. The Integral Test

If \(f(n)\) is a positive, continuous, and decreasing function for \(n \geq 1\) and \(a_n = f(n)\), then the series \( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n \) and the improper integral \( \int_{1}^{\infty} f(x) \, dx \) either both converge or both diverge.

Example:

Use the Integral Test to determine whether \( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2} \) converges.

Solution:

\int_{1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{x^2} \, dx = \left[ -\frac{1}{x} \right]_1^{\infty} = 1 \Rightarrow \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2} \text{ converges.}

5. The Comparison Test

If \(0 \leq a_n \leq b_n\) for all \(n\) and \( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} b_n \) converges, then \( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n \) also converges. If \( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n \) diverges, then \( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} b_n \) also diverges.

Example:

Determine if \( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n(n+1)} \) converges using the Comparison Test.

Solution:

\frac{1}{n(n+1)} < \frac{1}{n^2} \text{ and we know } \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2} \text{ converges.}
\Rightarrow \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n(n+1)} \text{ converges by Comparison Test.}

6. The Limit Comparison Test

Given two series \(\sum a_n\) and \(\sum b_n\) with positive terms, if \( \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{a_n}{b_n} = c \) where \(c\) is a positive and finite number, then either both series converge or both diverge.

Example:

Use the Limit Comparison Test to determine if \( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{n}{n^3 + 1} \) converges.

Solution:

\lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{\frac{n}{n^3 + 1}}{\frac{1}{n^2}} = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{n^3}{n^3 + 1} = 1
\Rightarrow \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{n}{n^3 + 1} \text{ converges by comparison to } \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2}.

7. Alternating Series Test

An alternating series is a series whose terms are alternately positive and negative. It converges if the absolute value of the terms decreases monotonically to 0.

Example:

Determine if the series \( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n} \) converges.

Solution:

The terms \frac{1}{n} decrease monotonically to 0.
\Rightarrow \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n} \text{ converges by the Alternating Series Test.}

8. The Ratio Test

For a series \(\sum a_n\), if \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \left| \frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n} \right| = L\): - If \(L < 1\), the series converges absolutely. - If \(L> 1\) or \(L = \infty\), the series diverges. - If \(L = 1\), the test is inconclusive.

Example:

Determine if the series \( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{n!}{3^n} \) converges using the Ratio Test.

Solution:

\lim_{n \to \infty} \left| \frac{(n+1)! / 3^{n+1}}{n! / 3^n} \right|
= \lim_{n \to \infty} \left| \frac{(n+1)}{3} \right| = \infty
\Rightarrow \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{n!}{3^n} \text{ diverges by the Ratio Test.}

9. The Root Test

For a series \(\sum a_n\), if \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \sqrt[n]{|a_n|} = L\): - If \(L < 1\), the series converges absolutely. - If \(L> 1\) or \(L = \infty\), the series diverges. - If \(L = 1\), the test is inconclusive.

Example:

Determine if the series \( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left( \frac{1}{2} \right)^n \) converges using the Root Test.

Solution:

\lim_{n \to \infty} \sqrt[n]{\left( \frac{1}{2} \right)^n} = \frac{1}{2}
\Rightarrow \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left( \frac{1}{2} \right)^n \text{ converges by the Root Test.}
