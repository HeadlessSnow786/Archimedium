AP Calculus BC - Unit 9: Parametric Equations, Polar Coordinates, and Vector-Valued Functions

1. Parameterizing Motion

Parametric equations describe motion by defining position as functions of time.

Example: Given \( x(t) = t^2 \) and \( y(t) = t + 1 \):

  • Find the position at \( t = 2 \).

Solution: \( x(2) = 2^2 = 4 \) and \( y(2) = 2 + 1 = 3 \). Thus, the position is \( (4, 3) \).

2. Calculus with Parametric Curves

To find the derivatives, use: 

dx/dt and dy/dt

Example: Given \( x(t) = t^2 \) and \( y(t) = t + 1 \), find dy/dx at \( t = 2 \).

  • First, compute dx/dt = 2t and dy/dt = 1.
  • Thus, \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{\frac{dy}{dt}}{\frac{dx}{dt}} = \frac{1}{2t} \text{ at } t=2 = \frac{1}{4}. \]

3. Polar Coordinates and Graphs

Polar coordinates use angles and radii. A point is defined as \( (r, \theta) \).

Example: Convert \( (3, \pi/4) \) to Cartesian coordinates.

  • Use \( x = r \cos(\theta) \) and \( y = r \sin(\theta) \).
  • Thus, \( x = 3 \cos(\pi/4) = 3 \times \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} = \frac{3\sqrt{2}}{2} \)
  • and \( y = 3 \sin(\pi/4) = 3 \times \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} = \frac{3\sqrt{2}}{2} \).

4. Derivatives of Polar Functions

Find \( \frac{dy}{dx} \) for polar functions \( r(\theta) \) using: \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{\frac{d}{d\theta} (r \sin(\theta))}{\frac{d}{d\theta} (r \cos(\theta))}. \]

Example: For \( r(\theta) = 2 + \theta \):

  • Find \( \frac{dy}{d\theta} = \frac{d}{d\theta}((2 + \theta) \sin(\theta)) \).
  • Apply the product rule: \[ \frac{dy}{d\theta} = (2 + \theta) \cos(\theta) + \sin(\theta) \]
  • And \( \frac{dx}{d\theta} = \frac{d}{d\theta}((2 + \theta) \cos(\theta)) \). \[ \frac{dx}{d\theta} = -(2 + \theta) \sin(\theta) + \cos(\theta) \]
  • Thus, \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{(2 + \theta) \cos(\theta) + \sin(\theta)}{-(2 + \theta) \sin(\theta) + \cos(\theta)} \]

5. Vector-Valued Functions

Express motion through vectors. The position vector \( \mathbf{r}(t) = \langle x(t), y(t) \rangle \).

Example: Given \( \mathbf{r}(t) = \langle t^2, t + 1 \rangle \):

  • Find velocity \(\mathbf{v}(t)\): it's the derivative of \( \mathbf{r}(t) \). \(\mathbf{v}(t) = \langle 2t, 1 \rangle \).
  • Find the acceleration \(\mathbf{a}(t)\): it's the derivative of \( \mathbf{v}(t) \). \(\mathbf{a}(t) = \langle 2, 0 \rangle \).

6. Speed, Arc Length, and Curvature

Speed is the magnitude of the velocity vector: \( \| \mathbf{v}(t) \| = \sqrt{(dx/dt)^2 + (dy/dt)^2} \).

Arc length from \( a \) to \( b \) is: \[ \int_a^b \sqrt{\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{dy}{dt}\right)^2} dt. \]

Example: Given \( x(t) = t^2 \) and \( y(t) = t + 1 \) from \( t=0 \) to \( t=1 \):

  • Find speed: \( \sqrt{(2t)^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{4t^2 + 1} \).
  • Arc length: \[ \int_0^1 \sqrt{4t^2 + 1} dt. \]
