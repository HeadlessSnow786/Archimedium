Unit 4: Contextual Applications of Differentiation

4.1 Interpreting the Meaning of the Derivative in Context

The derivative can be interpreted in various contexts such as rate of change, velocity, and other practical applications. It tells us how a function is changing at any given point.

Example: If s(t) represents the position of an object at time t, then s'(t) represents the velocity of the object at time t.

Solution: Given s(t) = t^2 + 3t, find the velocity at t = 2.
s'(t) = 2t + 3
s'(2) = 2(2) + 3 = 7

4.2 Straight-Line Motion: Connecting Position, Velocity, and Acceleration

The position function s(t) describes the location of an object, the first derivative s'(t) (velocity) describes the rate of change of position, and the second derivative s''(t) (acceleration) describes the rate of change of velocity.

Example: Given s(t) = t^3 − 6t^2 + 9t, find the velocity and acceleration at t = 2.

Solution:

  • Velocity: s'(t) = 3t^2 − 12t + 9
    s'(2) = 3(2)^2 − 12(2) + 9 = −3
  • Acceleration: s''(t) = 6t − 12
    s''(2) = 6(2) − 12 = 0

4.3 Rates of Change in Applied Contexts Other Than Motion

The derivative can represent rates of change in contexts other than motion, such as growth rates, rates of decay, or any other changing quantity.

Example: If P(t) represents the population of a town at time t, then P'(t) represents the growth rate of the population.

Solution: Given P(t) = 100e^(0.02t), find the growth rate at t = 5.
P'(t) = 2e^(0.02t)
P'(5) = 2e^(0.1) ≈ 2.21

4.4 Introduction to Related Rates

Related rates problems involve finding the rates at which related quantities change by differentiating with respect to time.

Example: If a circle’s radius is increasing at a rate of 3 cm/s, how fast is the area increasing when the radius is 10 cm?

Solution:

  • Given: dr/dt = 3 cm/s
  • Area of circle: A = πr^2
  • Differentiate: dA/dt = 2πr (dr/dt)
  • At r = 10: dA/dt = 2π(10)(3) = 60π cm^2/s

4.5 Solving Related Rates Problems

Follow these steps to solve related rates problems:

  1. Identify the quantities that change and their rates of change.
  2. Express the given information in equation form.
  3. Use implicit differentiation to find the relationship between rates of change.
  4. Substitute given values to solve for the desired rate.

Example: Water is poured into an inverted cone at a rate of 2 cubic meters per minute. If the height and base radius of the cone are always equal, how fast is the water level rising when the height is 3 meters?

Solution:

  • Given: dV/dt = 2 m^3/min, h = r
  • Volume of a cone: V = (1/3)πr^2h
  • With h = r, V = (1/3)πh^3
  • Differentiate: dV/dt = πh^2(dh/dt)
  • At h = 3: 2 = π(3)^2(dh/dt)
    2 = 9π(dh/dt)
    dh/dt = 2/(9π) m/min

4.6 Linearization and Local Linearity

Linearization uses the tangent line at a point to approximate the value of the function near that point.

Example: Find the linearization of f(x) = √x at x = 4 and use it to approximate √3.9.

Solution:

  • f'(x) = (1/2)x^(-1/2)
  • At x = 4: f(4) = 2 and f'(4) = 1/4
  • Tangent line: y - 2 = (1/4)(x - 4)
    y = (1/4)x + 1
  • Approximation: f(3.9) ≈ (1/4)(3.9) + 1 = 1.975

4.7 L'Hospital’s Rule

L'Hopital's Rule helps resolve indeterminate forms like 0/0 or ∞/∞ by differentiating the numerator and denominator.

Example: Evaluate lim(x→0) (sin x)/x.

Solution:

  • Indeterminate form: 0/0
  • Apply L'Hopital's Rule: lim(x→0) (cos x)/1 = cos(0) = 1
